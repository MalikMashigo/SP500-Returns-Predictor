# Semester Project: S&P 500 Short-Term Price Movement Prediction

## Part 2: Datasets

### Data Sources

For this project, I need data that captures the behavior of the S&P 500 across a wide range of market conditions including bull runs, crashes, recoveries, and everything in between. My primary dataset is S&P 500 historical OHLCV data (open, high, low, close prices, and trading volume) downloaded through the `yfinance` Python library using the ticker `^GSPC`, covering January 2, 2005 through December 31, 2024, which comes out to roughly 5,030 trading days. The yfinance library documentation is available at [https://pypi.org/project/yfinance/](https://pypi.org/project/yfinance/).

Alongside the primary dataset, I downloaded three secondary datasets. The first is the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX, ticker `^VIX`) from Yahoo Finance, which captures market-wide fear and uncertainty as a contextual feature. The second is U.S. Treasury yield data for both the 3-month and 10-year instruments, sourced from the Federal Reserve's FRED database at [https://fred.stlouisfed.org/](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/). The third is closing prices for five sector ETFs representing XLK (Technology), XLF (Financials), XLV (Healthcare), XLE (Energy), and XLP (Consumer Staples), also pulled from Yahoo Finance. All of this data has been physically downloaded and is stored locally.

### Dataset Splits

One of the most important design decisions for this project is how to split the data into training, validation, and test subsets. With financial time series, you absolutely cannot do a random shuffle the way you might with image data or text classification. If you did, the model would end up training on data from 2022 and validating on data from 2015, meaning it would have already seen the future during training. This problem is called look-ahead bias and it would make any reported accuracy completely meaningless in practice. Instead, I am doing a strict temporal split where the training set covers January 2005 through December 2018 (approximately 3,520 trading days, or about 70% of the data), the validation set covers January 2019 through December 2021 (roughly 756 days, about 15%), and the test set covers January 2022 through December 2024 (roughly 754 days, another 15%). The test partition has been downloaded and set aside and will not be examined until the final evaluation.

### Differences Between Subsets

The reason these three subsets are meaningfully different from each other comes down to the fact that financial markets are non-stationary. The statistical properties of returns, volatility, and feature correlations all shift over time, sometimes dramatically, so each subset deliberately reflects a different market environment.

The training set spans a wide variety of regimes including the pre-crisis bull market, the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the slow recovery of 2010 through 2013, and the extended low-volatility bull market that followed. This diversity is important because it gives the model exposure to many different types of conditions rather than letting it memorize a single regime. The validation set is deliberately chosen to be more turbulent and structurally different from training. It includes the final phase of the longest bull market in U.S. history in 2019, the COVID-19 crash and one of the fastest recoveries ever recorded in 2020, and the stimulus-fueled surge of 2021, a period where the VIX hit 82 which is something the model has never seen during training. The validation set will be used to tune hyperparameters like learning rate, sequence length, dropout rate, and LSTM depth without contaminating the test set. The test set covering 2022 through 2024 includes the Fed's aggressive rate-hiking cycle, the 2022 bear market driven by inflation, and the AI-driven bull rally of 2023 and 2024. This is the most out-of-distribution period relative to training, which makes it the closest thing to a real-world stress test I can construct.

### Sample Characterization

Each model input is a sliding window of 30 consecutive trading days of features used to predict a label for day 31. The raw features per day include the OHLCV values, VIX close, 3-month and 10-year treasury yields, and closing prices for the five sector ETFs. On top of those, I compute several engineered features including simple and exponential moving averages over 5, 10, 20, and 50 day windows, RSI-14, MACD and its signal line, Bollinger Bands and bandwidth, daily log returns, and rolling 20-day realized volatility. Since this is daily market data, the sampling frequency is one record per U.S. trading day. Weekends and holidays are naturally absent and I am not interpolating across them.

Labels are generated by computing the 1-day forward log return and bucketing it into three classes: UP (return greater than +0.2%), DOWN (return less than -0.2%), or FLAT (within the ±0.2% band). The ±0.2% threshold was chosen to produce roughly balanced classes based on the training set's return distribution, though some imbalance remains. UP days occur about 52% of the time, DOWN about 38%, and FLAT about 10%. This imbalance will be addressed through class weighting in the loss function.

### Data Samples

To give a sense of what the data looks like across the three subsets, here are three representative examples. From the training set, on September 15, 2008 (the day Lehman Brothers collapsed), the S&P 500 opened at 1251.7, closed at 1192.7, and the VIX was at 31.7, giving a label of DOWN. From the validation set, on March 16, 2020 (the height of the COVID crash), the index opened at 2409.4 and closed at 2386.1 with a VIX of 82.7, also labeled DOWN. From the test set, on June 13, 2022 (during the Fed rate hike period), the index opened at 3813.1 and closed at 3749.6 with a VIX of 34.0, labeled DOWN.

These three examples all happen to be significant down days, but they illustrate something important: the same VIX level of around 34 appeared in both 2008 and 2022, but those periods had completely different interest rate environments, sector dynamics, and economic contexts. This is exactly why the model is fed sequences of multi-feature data rather than just price and volatility alone. It needs enough context to distinguish between superficially similar situations that have very different underlying dynamics.
